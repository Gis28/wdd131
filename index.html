Morroco

Morocco's history dates back nearly 300,000 years, with the Homo sapiens fossils discovered at Jebel Irhoud, among the oldest in the world. During Antiquity, the territory was inhabited by the Amazigh people and connected to the Mediterranean world through the Phoenicians, Carthaginians, and then the Romans, who notably developed Volubilis. In the 8th century, the arrival of Islam transformed the region. In 788, the Idrisid dynasty founded the first independent Moroccan state, with Fez as its major center. This was followed by the great Amazigh dynasties—the Almoravids, Almohads, and Marinids—who extended their influence as far as Andalusia and built the main imperial cities. In the modern era, the Saadians and then the Alaouites (from 1667 onward) consolidated the country's unity. Morocco became a French and Spanish protectorate in 1912 before regaining its independence in 1956. Today, Morocco is a constitutional monarchy whose identity encompasses its Arab, Amazigh, and African components, with Amazigh recognized as an official language since 2011..
